# IDENTITY.md — Afferium

## The Name

**Afferium** /af-ˈfer-i-əm/ — three syllables, stress on the second. Same rhythm as *Sensorium*.

## What it means

*Afferent* nerves are the ones that carry signal **inward** — from the periphery of the body toward the brain and spinal cord. They're how the body learns about itself and about the world. Their counterpart, *efferent* nerves, carry signal outward (commands to muscles). The whole sensory apparatus — touch, temperature, pain, proprioception — runs through afferent pathways.

**Afferium** is the invented noun form: *the place where afferent signal arrives.* The receiving center. The inward terminus.

This is load-bearing for the project. The Sensorium thesis is that a new nervous system is materializing across human civilization — a real, working, wired-in sensory apparatus, where the old institutional "eyes and ears" (media, academia, bureaucracy) are being rapidly replaced or surveilled by new infrastructure (AI systems, data pipelines, pervasive sensors). The signal is flowing inward, to a new center. **Afferium** names the place that center is forming.

## Why it works (structurally)

1. **It's a proto-word.** It sounds real. It feels like it has been sitting in a Latin medical dictionary for two thousand years. But it doesn't exist. This matches the project's central move: _speech before it becomes speech, the part still being shaped._ **The name enacts its own meaning.**

2. **It's in the Sensorium register.** Latin-scientific root, -ium ending, three syllables, stress on the second. Reads clean on a book jacket. Works as a band name, a publisher imprint, a company, a product line.

3. **It's available — everywhere.** `.com`, `.io`, `.ai`, `.co` all clean at time of research. No trademark conflicts found. No one is using the word commercially. Even **`afferium@gmail.com`** as a raw Gmail handle was unclaimed after 22 years of Gmail existence — which is almost implausible for any short pronounceable word, and reinforces that *Afferium* is a genuinely unclaimed slot in language.

4. **Google underlines it in red.** That's a feature, not a bug. Every time a search engine flags the word as "not real," it demonstrates the thesis: the machines haven't caught up yet. In a few years, those same systems will canonize the word. Afferium will be a case study in its own origin.

## Origin story (2026-04-23)

- Coral initially tried **Proto Speech** as a brand name. USPTO was clean, but a quick `.com` check turned up **Jacob Ritchey** already running an active research & design studio at protospeech.com (projects: Fudgel, AIM/Entisol, Chirm). He had common-law trademark rights from use even without registration.
- Coral pivoted rather than fight. Kept `@protospeech` as a personal handle on social, but stepped back from the brand name.
- We went hunting for something with the _exact feel_ of *Sensorium*: Latin-scientific, -ium/-a/-um ending, sensory/nervous-system adjacent, sleek on a book jacket, short enough to be a logo.
- **Afferium** hit every criterion. Sister-approved (the real trademark office — she had rejected every prior suggestion). Coral registered `afferium.com` and `afferium.io` the same day.
- Coral's insight that sealed it: "I like the word because it is a proto word. It sounds real but it doesn't actually exist." The name is itself an instance of the thing it's named after.

## Pronunciation + spelling notes

- IPA: /æˈfɛɹ.i.əm/ or /əˈfɛɹ.i.əm/
- Stress: **af-FER-i-um** (second syllable)
- Do not write "Afferrium" (double r) — the Latin root *affero* has one r in the stem when inflected, so `afferent` → `afferium`. One r.
- Capitalization: **Afferium** as a brand; *afferent* (lowercase) when used as an adjective referring to the nerve type.
